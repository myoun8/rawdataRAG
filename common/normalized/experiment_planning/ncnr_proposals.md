# User Proposals

All proposals must be submitted via the web-based NCNR-IMS database application. Log on to your NCNR-IMS account (https://ncnrims.nist.gov/); follow the same link to create an account if you don't have one. Internal beamtime requests must be submitted in the same manner.

For detailed information on proposal preparation, proposal review, instrument availability, and facility access, read the instructions posted for the latest Call for Proposals (https://www.nist.gov/ncnr/call-proposals).

Beam time is provided without charges, except for proprietary research. Two programs can provide some monetary assistance (travel and subsistence support) for new users of the NCNR (https://www.nist.gov/ncnr/accessing-ncnr/financial-assistance): one administered by the University of Maryland for new users from US universities with approved experiments, and a second sponsored by the National Science Foundation that provides travel funds for new graduate students to introduce them to neutron scattering at the NCNR.

## Proposal instructions

1. Proposal types:
   - A **New Proposal** is a regular proposal for instrument time. Continuation proposals no longer comprise a separate class. All proposals are reviewed by experts in their subject matter and by the Beam Time Allocation Committee (BTAC).
   - A **Quick Access Proposal** asks for beam time in the very near future for measurements that cannot be delayed. Such a proposal is reviewed by the BTAC and held to a higher standard than regular proposals.
   - A **Beam Time Request** is not a proposal, but a request for part of the instrument time reserved for NIST internal research programs or for Participating Research Teams. Under certain conditions, external users may be able to submit such a request (https://www.nist.gov/ncnr/beamtime-requests-vs-proposals).
2. Preferred dates for the performance of an experiment, and/or impossible dates, should be indicated.
3. Indicate the name of the person to be contacted in all future communication regarding the proposal, who will be assumed to be the first name on the list of proposers unless otherwise indicated.
4. Identify graduate students and indicate whether the proposed work is an essential part of a student's thesis.
5. If a proposal is successful and no suggested local contact has been identified, the NCNR will choose one.
6. Standard sample environments vary according to instrument. Examine the instrument descriptions and the sample environment pages (https://ncnr.nist.gov/equipment/ancequip.html) for more information. If you require a non-standard sample environment, contact the appropriate instrument scientist to discuss feasibility.
7. **Safety is a consideration of prime importance at NIST.** All safety rules and standard practices observed at NIST and the NCNR must be followed by guest researchers. Your proposal will receive a safety review by NIST staff. If you are aware of any possible hazards associated with your proposed experiment, describe these in detail. Failure to do so may delay or cancel your experiment.
8. Consult the more detailed instructions (https://www.nist.gov/ncnr/planning-your-experiment/web-proposal-form) and the proposal help page (https://www.nist.gov/ncnr/explanation-ncnr-proposal-form-fields) for in-depth information on accessing and filling out the form line by line.

## Tips on Proposal Writing

Proposals are evaluated on the basis of scientific merit and technical soundness by external reviewers and BTAC members. External reviewers may not be experts in neutron beam methods, although they will be experts in the scientific areas of the proposals they review. BTAC members are generally knowledgeable about neutron methods but may be less familiar with your scientific area. A review of technical feasibility by an NCNR staff member is also carried out.

Advice:

1. **Place your proposal in a reasonably broad scientific context.** Do not assume reviewers and BTAC members will appreciate the significance of your proposed experiment if you do not state it. At the same time, overstatements are inadvisable.
2. **Indicate clearly what is to be measured and how.** If some or all of your samples are already prepared, make this clear; otherwise it will be assumed they are not yet ready. Provide enough detail about the measurements to justify the requested beam time.
3. **Proposals should be reasonably self-contained.** Do not rely on cited references as an essential part of your case. Mail reviewers may look up some references, but BTAC members are unlikely to do so.

The SANS subcommittee of the BTAC emphasizes that, if possible, proposals should include the following in the appropriate fields on the proposal form:

- experimental details such as specific compositions and concentrations
- anticipated run times and desired Q-ranges
- scientific background and anticipated impact
- anticipated method of data analysis
- list of publications from prior NCNR instrument time
